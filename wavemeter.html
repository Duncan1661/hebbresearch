Wavemeter – Type 174-B (1918–1920)

A compact, oak-cased absorption wavemeter built in the early 20th century for accurately tuning and measuring radio frequencies between 260 to 1040 meters (approx. 290–1150 kHz).

(outside look)

The Type 174-B Wavemeter, manufactured by the General Radio Company in the late 1910s, is a self-contained, direct-reading instrument used for detecting and measuring radio wavelengths. Designed during the infancy of wireless communication, it features a built-in crystal detector and hot-wire meter, allowing users to find resonance points by observing deflections caused by absorption. When the internal LC circuit resonates with an external transmitter's frequency, the meter shows a dip or peak, allowing the user to read the wavelength directly from the calibrated dial. This instrument helped early radio operators calibrate transmitters and check for harmonics or interference, an essential part of early radio development.

(inside look)

Physical Make

The instrument is housed in a medium-toned hardwood (likely oak) box with dovetail joints, a top-mounted rotating dial with frequency markings, a side-mounted buzzer switch, a hot-wire meter, and binding posts for signal input/output. Also having a closing lid and a leather strap for easy and safe transport.

Materials

  • Box: Hardwood oak (external housing)
  • Windows: Glass, allowing view of the wavelength scale and the galvanometer
  • Top Panel: Ebonite or Bakelite with engraved scales
  • Internal Components: Copper inductors, mica capacitors, early carbon or galena crystal detector
  • Hot-Wire Meter: Fine wire filament and magnet assembly housed in a small glass or metal casing
  • Battery Compartment: Originally fitted for an Eveready 771 (4.5V) battery
  • Knobs & Terminals: Made of Bakelite, brass, and steel screws

Dimensions

  • Length: 9.75 inches
  • Width: 7.5 inches
  • Height: 7 inches
  • Weight: Approximately 9 pounds

Markings

  • Wavelength Meter: Outer circle (0–100), middle circle (1000–3000), inner circle (400–1000). Numbers 1, 2, and 3 are written vertically on the side of the wavemeter's circular glass window.
  • Brass Plaque: Serial number and manufacturer details: “General Radio Company – Cambridge, Mass – Type 174-B”
  • Buzzers: Labeled “Buzzers,” “Switch Off,” “Adjust,” and “Range”
  • Wavemeter Knob: Labeled with the direction to “Increase Wavelength”
  • Hot-Wire Meter: Labeled with sensitivity units (0–100), with text below reading: “Hot Wire Galvanometer Model 127 No. 14773 General Radio Company Cambridge Mass”
  • Terminals: Labeled “Exit Coil” and “TEL”
  • Internal Lead: Includes a diagram indicating how to properly connect the wavemeter

Measurements

The wavemeter measures the wavelength (or frequency) of an incoming radio signal. To use it, the user tunes the internal dial to find the point of resonance—indicated by a visible change in the meter. The corresponding wavelength (in meters) is then read directly from the dial.

Manufacturer's Mark

Manufacturer and model: Wavemeter Type 174B, serial number 569, General Radio Company, Cambridge, Mass.

Functionality and Applications

The 174-B functions by employing a resonant LC circuit tuned to specific wavelengths. When this circuit comes into resonance with an incoming RF signal, the circuit absorbs energy, and the hot-wire ammeter indicates a change. Operators would vary the tuning dial and look for a dip or peak in the meter, indicating resonance. It was used in early radio telegraphy and engineering labs to calibrate transmitters and receivers, check harmonics, or analyze unknown signals in the MW (medium wave) range. A small built-in buzzer provided an audible tone to help identify tuning peaks in absence of a meter signal.

Instrument physics

The 174-B operates on the principle of resonance in an LC (inductor-capacitor) circuit. When the natural frequency of the circuit equals the frequency of the external signal, resonance occurs, resulting in maximum energy transfer. This causes increased current in the detector circuit, heating the wire in the hot-wire ammeter (which measures current via thermal expansion), or triggering the crystal detector. The resonance point corresponds to a specific wavelength, which the user reads from the calibrated dial. This passive, non-radiating method made it valuable in tuning and analyzing transmitters without interfering with ongoing transmissions.

Manufacturer's Information

The General Radio Company (GenRad) e was founded in 1915 in Cambridge, Massachusetts, and became a pioneer in precision measurement instruments during the golden age of radio. Its early catalogues (1918–1925) feature components such as wavemeters, oscillators, and audio equipment. GenRad remained active until 2001, when it was acquired by Teradyne, and later merged into QuadTech, which continued producing test instruments. Today, GenRad equipment is considered historically important in the evolution of metrology, RF engineering, and electronics manufacturing.

Reflection From The Present

Although devices like the 174-B have long been replaced by digital frequency counters and spectrum analyzers, the principles they demonstrated—resonance, passive tuning, and non-invasive measurement—are still core to RF electronics today. Looking at this instrument reminds me how engineers once relied on pure physics and analog craftsmanship, not screens and chips. The 174-B was built in a time when radio was new and unpredictable, so the rugged wood casing, large dials, and direct feedback weren’t just design choices—they were solutions for working in unstandardized, early environments. In a world of handheld digital meters, this feels like holding the origin story of radio engineering.
